Alexa-Priv

Rethinking the Smart Speakers of tomorrow through
Experimental Design

Project Overview

Motivation

Voice-based smart assistants pose novel privacy risks since these devices are always listening for activation keywords. The privacy concern with such a device is two-fold. First, the device itself can be compromised to eavesdrop on the users. Second, these devices can learn the user’s preferences and can invade privacy by providing targeted ads and other services beyond what users expect or want the smart assistant to learn about them.

In order to tackle this problem current solutions have certain levels of privacy controls integrated into their system such as activation keyword detection, physical mute button and speaker recognition (Google Home). However, the mute button present in current devices such as Amazon echo does likely not provide an intuitive control for privacy. Users may forget to mute the device before saying something sensitive or the device may be muted when they want to use it.

Objective

We propose to leverage interpersonal communication cues, such as people’s tendency to look at their conversation partner or modulating how loudly they speaking depending on who you should hear them, to design more natural and effective privacy controls for voice-based smart assistants.

Hypothesis

The proposed design when compared to current privacy controls, will lower privacy concerns and increase the perceived privacy without negatively affecting the user experience or utility of the smart speakers.

Note:

This research is still in progress and hence specific details about the research is removed and a generic approach is presented. The research will be presented at the Data Transparency Lab at Barcelona in December and the paper will also be published at ACM Journal such as CHI or UbiComp.

Study Design

Recruitment

Participants are recruited by sending recruitment emails in list servers and public forums such as Facebook and Craigslist. Flyers are posted around the campus of the University and around the city of Ann Arbor.

The user base for our study can be either existing users or non-users of voice based smart assistants. By inviting participants from a variety of settings such as students and others from within the city, the sample is equitable and representative of a larger collection of user population.

Variables and Conditions

The following is a list of variables that is crucial for the study design and evaluation.

  • Control Variable: For the study, the existing amazon alexa device is used as the control device with its “mute button” being the control condition

  • Independent Variables: For the study 3 levels of intervention techniques were developed as test conditions and is tested against the control condition.

  • Dependent Variables: The different conditions are tested with different “within-subjects” as a pair and a list of 4 dependent variable is measured

  • Random Variable: 
    - Expertise in using smart speakers
    - Extroverted/ Introverted nature of participants

  • Confounding Variables:
    - Position placement of the smart speakers
    - Lighting Conditions
    - Complexity of the tasks presented
    - Skill transfer at the end of each tasks.

Order Effects

With 4 independent test conditions and user tasks, there is a possibility to get biased results due to the order in which the tasks and conditions are tested.

To account for this order effect Orthogonal Balanced Latin square is used as a counterbalance technique. This determines the order in which the tasks needs to be presented to the participants.

Each group is a pair of participants (friends) who will be taking the tasks in order represented in the table.

Evaluation 

User Tasks

Participants will asked to evaluate 4 conditions in order as specified by the latin square. Each condition is paired up with a user task asking the participants to engage in a discussion involving Alexa.

  • Condition A: This is the Control condition and represents the current interaction method of existing smart speakers.

  • Condition B: This is an intervening condition using gaze to detect the need for the smart speaker

  • Condition C: This is an intervening condition using ambient volume to detect the need for the speaker

  • Condition D: This is an intervening condition using both gaze and volume.

Interview

After the completion of each task, a brief interview is conducted to qualitatively understand the participant's perceived privacy towards the system. The interview questions are open ended and targets the participant's emotions and to understand “why” the participant did something as opposed to something else.

SUS Questionnaire

The System Usability Scale (SUS) is a list of 10 questions asked to identify the usability of a system under investigation. This scale is used to evaluate quantitatively the usability of the designed system. 

This Questionnair is presented at the end of each task.

Emotion Coding

At the end of each task, the participants are presented with the Self-Assessment Manikin (SAM) and are asked to select the emoton that closely relates to theirs while using the system. 

This technique is used to analyze and understand their emotion towards the system.

Card Sorting

Card sorting is conduced after the study is completed. Each of the participants are asked to rank the different conditions in their order of preference. Once ranked, they will be asked to compare their ranking and discuss about it.
